Overview


Photonic crystal fibres (PCFs) underpin critical applications in telecommunications, sensing, and high-power laser delivery, yet their design remains a manual, expert-intensive process that couples geometric intuition with deep waveguide physics. Vision-language models (VLMs) offer a promising path toward automating this pipeline, but can they truly reason about photonic physics from microscopy images, or do they merely recognise visual patterns?

We introduce PCFBench, the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating VLMs on the complete photonic inverse-design workflow. PCFBench features: (i) 26 tasks organised into six categories of increasing cognitive demand — Geometry Perception, Physics Understanding, Text Generation, Multi-modal Reasoning, Inverse Design, and Code Generation; (ii) data drawn from over 220K FDTD simulation samples across 18 structurally diverse fibre families; and (iii) evaluation of both individual VLMs on isolated tasks and multi-agent systems (MAS) on the full sequential pipeline from perception to design.

Our results reveal a striking perception-design gap: current models can identify fibre geometry with moderate success, yet consistently fail to translate that visual understanding into physics-aware design actions. Cascading agents through the pipeline exposes significant error propagation, where upstream perception mistakes compound into downstream design failures. These findings suggest that neither model scaling nor naive agent chaining suffices; closing the loop demands explicit physical grounding.

PCFBench is designed with several key features: (i) Physics-grounded. Every task is anchored in FDTD-validated optical properties (loss, dispersion, mode area, birefringence) so that "correct" answers correspond to physically realisable fibres. (ii) Multi-modal. Models must jointly reason over cross-section microscopy-style structure images, electromagnetic field maps, and structured text prompts. (iii) Agentic. Beyond per-task scoring, PCFBench evaluates multi-agent systems on the full perception → physics → design pipeline, exposing how early errors propagate. (iv) Diverse. 18 fibre families — including hollow-core, anti-resonant, photonic bandgap, kagome, dual-coupler, and chirped designs — ensure the benchmark stresses generalisation rather than family memorisation.

Benchmark Design

Six Dimensions, 26 Tasks

1. Geometry Perception

Reads structural features directly from cross-section images. Tasks include family classification, hole counting, topology analysis, pitch & hole-diameter regression, and defect localisation.

2. Physics Understanding

Tests reasoning about optical properties. Tasks include confinement-loss prediction at 1550 nm, application identification, mechanism keyword retrieval, and dispersion-regime classification.

3. Text Generation

Generates structured natural-language artefacts: geometry descriptions, physics QA, and full design reports, evaluated with BERTScore-F1 and an LLM-judged completeness rubric.

4. Multi-modal Reasoning

Forces joint use of structure and field images via paired matching: wavelength-conditioned matching (WC) and mode-discrimination matching (MD).

5. Inverse Design

Goes from target spec to candidate fibre. Tasks: directional pitch / hole-diameter editing, parameter selection, closed-loop FDTD success, and human-rated design quality.

6. Code Generation

Produces runnable artefacts: simulation-code quality, parametric-model synthesis, and refactor quality, scored against an executable physics oracle.

Key Findings

Finding 1. Perception–Design gap. Even the strongest frontier VLMs identify fibre geometry with reasonable accuracy yet fail to translate that perception into physics-consistent design actions — closed-loop FDTD success drops by >30 points relative to perception accuracy.

Finding 2. Scaling helps perception but plateaus on physics. Within open-source families, larger models improve geometry recognition steadily, while loss-prediction error and inverse-design success show diminishing returns past 30B parameters.

Finding 3. Multi-agent pipelines amplify upstream errors. Cascading specialised agents through perception → physics → design propagates early geometry mistakes into compounding downstream failures, exposing the brittleness of naive agent chaining.

Finding 4. Closed loops beat open ones. Adding an executable FDTD oracle in the loop — even one as cheap as a single mode-solver call — recovers a substantial fraction of the perception–design gap, suggesting explicit physical grounding is necessary.
