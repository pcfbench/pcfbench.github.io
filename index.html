01 — Overview

A physics-grounded test for VLMs on photonic design.

Photonic crystal fibres power telecommunications, sensing and high-power laser delivery — but designing them is still expert-driven. Can VLMs reason about photonic physics from images, or just pattern-match?

We introduce PCFBench, the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating VLMs on the complete photonic inverse-design workflow. PCFBench features 26 tasks organised into six categories of increasing cognitive demand, drawn from over 220K FDTD simulation samples across 18 structurally diverse fibre families, and evaluates both individual VLMs on isolated tasks and multi-agent systems on the full perception → physics → design pipeline.

Our results reveal a striking perception-design gap: current models can identify fibre geometry with moderate success, yet consistently fail to translate that visual understanding into physics-aware design actions. Cascading agents amplifies the gap — upstream perception mistakes compound into downstream design failures. Closing the loop, our findings suggest, demands explicit physical grounding, not just scale.

02 — Benchmark Design

Six dimensions, 26 tasks.

Each dimension targets a distinct cognitive demand — from reading geometry off a cross-section image to writing executable simulation code.

01 / Perception

Geometry Perception

Reads structural features directly from cross-section images: family classification, hole counting, topology analysis, pitch & hole-diameter regression, defect localisation.

02 / Physics

Physics Understanding

Tests reasoning about optical properties: confinement-loss prediction at 1550 nm, application identification, mechanism keyword retrieval, dispersion-regime classification.

03 / Text

Text Generation

Generates structured language artefacts: geometry descriptions, physics QA, full design reports — scored with BERTScore-F1 and an LLM-judged completeness rubric.

04 / Multi-modal

Multi-modal Reasoning

Forces joint use of structure and field images via paired matching: wavelength-conditioned (WC) and mode-discrimination (MD).

05 / Design

Inverse Design

From target spec to candidate fibre: directional pitch / hole-diameter editing, parameter selection, closed-loop FDTD success, human-rated design quality.

06 / Code

Code Generation

Produces runnable artefacts: simulation-code quality, parametric-model synthesis, refactor quality — scored against an executable physics oracle.

04 — Key Findings

Scale alone doesn't close the loop.

Four observations from evaluating 41 VLMs across 26 tasks — perception is solved, design is not.

F1Perception–Design gap Even the strongest frontier VLMs identify fibre geometry with reasonable accuracy yet fail to translate that perception into physics-consistent design actions — closed-loop FDTD success drops by more than 30 points relative to perception accuracy.
F2Scaling plateaus on physics Within open-source families, larger models improve geometry recognition steadily, while loss-prediction error and inverse-design success show diminishing returns past 30B parameters.
F3Agent pipelines amplify errors Cascading specialised agents through perception → physics → design propagates early geometry mistakes into compounding downstream failures, exposing the brittleness of naive agent chaining.
F4Closed loops beat open ones Adding an executable FDTD oracle in the loop — even one as cheap as a single mode-solver call — recovers a substantial fraction of the gap, suggesting explicit physical grounding is necessary.
